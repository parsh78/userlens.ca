The situation

Customers booked once. And didn't come back.

A travel platform had solid acquisition numbers. First bookings were healthy. But repeat booking rates were below where the business needed them to be — and customer lifetime value wasn't growing the way the model required.

The product roadmap was full of feature ideas. New filters, redesigned search, better photo galleries. Leadership had a gut feeling that none of these would actually solve the retention problem — but they couldn't articulate why, and they didn't have evidence to push back with.

"We kept adding things. The retention numbers barely moved. At some point someone said — maybe we don't know what's actually driving people to come back. That's when we called UserLens."

The ask was clear: find out what was actually happening with customers between their first and second booking — and what the platform could do to close that gap without another feature sprint.

What UserLens did

Mapping the emotional arc of a customer journey.

UserLens ran a full customer journey engagement — tracking not just what customers did on the platform, but how they felt at each stage, what they remembered, and what made them either want to return or quietly drift to a competitor.

1

Longitudinal customer interviews

Spoke with customers at three points: before booking, immediately after, and several weeks later. Capturing the emotional arc — anticipation, experience, reflection — revealed patterns that single-session surveys completely missed.

2

Loyalty and loss moment mapping

Identified the specific moments across the journey where customers either deepened their relationship with the brand — or started looking elsewhere. Some of these moments weren't in the product at all. They were in the emails, the confirmations, the silence after a booking.

3

CX strategy brief + priority framework

Delivered a strategy brief that showed exactly which moments to protect, which to repair, and which were irrelevant to retention. Gave leadership a prioritised framework they could act on without needing to guess.

Key moments mapped

Built loyalty

The confirmation moment

Customers who received a well-timed, personalised post-booking message felt significantly more connected to the brand — and recalled it when deciding where to book next.

Destroyed it quietly

The post-trip silence

After returning from a trip, customers heard nothing. No follow-up, no acknowledgement. For many, that silence was when they mentally filed the brand under "fine, but forgettable."

Built loyalty

When something went wrong — and got fixed

Customers who experienced a problem that was handled quickly and personally became the most loyal segment. Recovery experiences outperformed smooth ones for long-term retention.

Destroyed it quietly

Generic follow-up emails

Promotional emails sent without reference to the customer's actual trip felt tone-deaf. Several interviewees mentioned unsubscribing after receiving an offer for the destination they'd just returned from.

The outcome

17% lift in engagement. The roadmap got a reset.

With a clear map of which moments mattered most to customers, the team didn't add features — they redesigned three existing touchpoints and introduced two new ones specifically around the loyalty and loss moments UserLens identified.

What the evidence made possible

  • 17% increase in time-in-app and measurable lift in return booking rate within two quarters
  • Three existing product touchpoints redesigned around real loyalty drivers
  • Post-trip communication strategy rebuilt — resulting in a 30% reduction in unsubscribe rates
  • Product roadmap reprioritised: two planned feature builds were deprioritised in favour of CX repairs that delivered faster ROI
  • Leadership had a customer journey map they could use to evaluate every future initiative against real emotional drivers

The brand didn't need more features. It needed to stop losing customers at the moments it didn't realise were important. Once those moments were visible and evidenced, the decisions became straightforward — and the results followed.
